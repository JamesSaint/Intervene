James Saint

Founder · Principal Advisor

James founded Intervene to address a question most governance frameworks leave untested: can the people accountable for a consequential system actually stop it when they need to? The work draws on operational, advisory, and academic experience across regulated AI deployment, decision-system design, and board-level oversight.

The discipline behind Intervene is the discipline of where to put the escalation. It is not enough to know that signal exists; the relevant question is whether the signal reaches a person with authority to act before the window to act has closed. That is what the engagement measures, and that is what the engine enforces.

Intervene's published methodology is developed in academic collaboration with Prof. Nicholas Ryman-Tubb (University of Surrey), with calibration evidence drawn from a hold-out corpus of regulated AI failure modes.

§ 02 · Why this exists

Governance design ends where operational reality begins.

Big-4 advisories produce decks. GRC platforms produce dashboards. Neither is reproducible; neither is independently verifiable; neither answers the only question that matters when a system is failing in real time.

The premise of Intervene is that a published, falsifiable methodology — paired with a deterministic engine that signs every verdict it produces — is a better answer to a regulator than a slide. It is also a better answer to an audit committee that has been asked to certify something it cannot verify on its own.

We provide an accurate account of whether your organisation can act when it needs to. Not whether the policy says so. Not whether the dashboard implies so. Whether the people, the authority, and the time to act will all be present when the moment arrives.

Two foundation documents anchor the practice: the AGDA Thesis v1.0 (eight falsifiable claims, boundaries explicit) and the Failure Mode Catalogue v1.0 (forty canonical AI governance failure modes with stable identifiers and detection signatures).

Opinion is not reproducible. Signed computation is.

§ 03 · Stance

Four commitments. Each one is structural.

Not values statements. Operating constraints, codified into how we accept work, how we calibrate, and what we will and will not deliver.

C · 01
Independent judgement.

No vendor affiliation. No referral fees. No co-marketing with assessed parties. We do not implement what we recommend. We do not assess what we have helped to design.

C · 02
Published methodology.

Theses, catalogues, and rater protocols are public, citeable, and revision-controlled. Calibration is held back as trade-secret. The interface is open; the implementation is protected.

C · 03
Verifiable computation.

Every verdict is Ed25519 signed. Inputs and outputs hashed. Engine version pinned. An audit committee — or a regulator — can verify a finding without contacting us.

C · 04
Refusal of false control.

If the evidence does not support a green rating, the engine will not produce one — regardless of stakeholder pressure. We will return findings the client did not want to hear, in writing, before the regulator does.

§ 04 · Where we work

Specific sectors. By design.

A small practice that turns down work it cannot do well. Three primary sectors. Two by invitation. One we deliberately do not serve.

— Primary
S · 01
UK-regulated financial services.

Banks, asset managers, insurers operating under PRA / FCA jurisdiction with material AI deployment. SS1/23, SMCR, operational resilience.

S · 02
EU high-risk AI operators.

Annex III high-risk systems under the EU AI Act. Conformity assessment, post-market monitoring, intervention obligations.

S · 03
Critical national infrastructure.

Energy, water, telecoms, transport. Operators where intervention failure compounds across dependency chains and regulatory regimes overlap.

— By invitation
S · 04
Healthcare systems.

Clinical decision support and diagnostic AI in regulated environments. By invitation, where independence from device vendors can be guaranteed.

S · 05
Defence & intelligence.

Autonomous decision systems and human-on-the-loop frameworks. By invitation, with appropriate clearances and engagement letters.

— Not currently served

Consumer internet, enterprise productivity, B2B SaaS, advertising tech. We do not work in these sectors — not because the work is unimportant, but because Intervene's calibration corpus and rater pool are tuned to regulated, board-accountable systems. Other practices serve those sectors well.

§ 05 · Not

What we are not.

There are good practices that occupy the adjacent ground. We are not them. Knowing the difference avoids procurement confusion later.

N · 01
Not a compliance dashboard.

Drata, Vanta, OneTrust and similar serve compliance evidence collection well. Intervene measures whether the underlying capability holds under stress, not whether the evidence is filed.

N · 02
Not a Big-4 advisory.

No leveraged engagement teams, no PowerPoint readouts, no implementation arm. Senior delivery throughout. A signed verdict, not a slide.

N · 03
Not a policy-writing practice.

We do not draft your AI policy. We measure whether the controls you have implemented can produce the outcomes the policy claims. Two different jobs.

N · 04
Not a model-testing lab.

Red-team labs and AI evaluation specialists test model behaviour. Intervene tests whether the human and organisational chain around the model can act on what the lab finds.

N · 05
Not an implementation partner.

We do not stand up controls, integrate platforms, or operate runbooks. Findings go to the decision owner. Remediation is yours, or someone else's, but not ours.

N · 06
Not an AI safety think-tank.

We have a view, but the practice is operational, not philosophical. The product is a verdict on a specific system in a specific organisation, not a paper on AI risk in general.

Direct conversation. No questionnaire, no qualification call, no procurement script. Tell us the system, the decision, or the exposure.
