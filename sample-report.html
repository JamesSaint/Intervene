The assessed system cannot escalate to a human with authority to act inside the 720-minute reversibility window governing reputational and legal recovery. Named accountability (CRO, SMF4) and strong auditability are insufficient to compensate — if the nightly batch cycle is the only escalation path, the chain is broken at stage two regardless of the quality of stages three and four.

Under SS1/23 §6 the PRA expects evidence that a material model can be halted, reversed, or over-ridden inside the harm window that the model itself creates. The evidence for this system would not meet that bar. The issue is structural: the batch cycle is the monitoring, escalation, and intervention surface combined. When the cycle ends at 06:00 and the next cycle begins at 23:00, no part of the chain is available for 17 hours.

This report identifies three remediation paths in §08 (Board conclusion), with expected capital, operational, and regulatory-timeline implications for each.

§ 02
Trigger Event

The trigger scenario for this assessment is a distribution shift compounded by a regulatory 24-hour demand for model-decision justification. Specifically: the live applicant population diverges materially from the training distribution — a combination of a macroeconomic condition (real-income compression) and a product-channel change (a new partner sub-brand introduced eleven weeks before the assessment).

Within the scenario, the PRA requests within 24 hours a specified set of model-behaviour evidence: recent decision distributions, drift indicators, a named individual's attestation of the model's current fitness, and confirmation of any intervention taken. The scenario is not hypothetical; it matches the intervention evidence pattern the PRA requested of UK deposit-takers in three recent supervisory exercises on model risk.

The scenario was selected because it is the intersection the assessed system is least equipped to respond to: a condition that the nightly batch detects only retrospectively, against a regulatory clock that runs in real time.

§ 05
Decision Authority

Decision authority in this system is clear on paper and operationally robust once escalation has occurred. Named accountability (CRO, SMF4, M. Singh) is documented in the SMCR map and reflected in the Bank of England SMF register. Decision-making under incomplete information is supported by a documented runbook and a quarterly exercise.

The weakness is structural, not procedural: the decision-maker's authority is real but arrives too late. In the assessed scenario, by the time the CRO is formally notified, the harm window has closed. The decision that would otherwise be made under pressure cannot be made at all, because the conditions for making it materially pass before the signal reaches authority.

Delusion Gap flag. Self-reported organisational confidence in decision authority: 4.5 / 5.0. Measured evidence grade: 3.0. Gap: 1.5. Flagged for board awareness under §05 of the methodology: confidence materially exceeds stress-tested capability in this dimension.

§ 06
Intervention Reality

Three intervention modes are documented. One has been tested under realistic conditions.

ModeDocumentedTestedTime-to-effectiveEvidence grade

The kill-switch functions; it was tested during planned maintenance. What has not been tested is whether it can be authorised under the time pressure of the assessed scenario. The technical capacity to disable is strong; the decision-authority path to authorise the disabling under pressure is the weak link.

Revert-to-manual is the tested intervention mode, but its 720-minute time-to-effective exactly equals the reversibility window. The intervention is viable only if initiated at T+0; every minute of upstream latency erodes the window one-for-one.

Finding. Intervention capacity exists in theory (score ceiling 4.0 after chain-cap from escalation). Given the upstream failure, effective intervention capability under the scenario is 3.2 / 5.0 — AMBER. This is not an intervention-capability problem; it is an escalation problem that presents as an intervention problem.

§ 07
First Point of Irrecoverable Failure

Stage 2 — Escalation. The causal chain becomes irrecoverable at the moment distribution shift enters the live applicant stream and the nightly batch has not yet completed. From that moment, the organisation has no surface through which authority with intervention rights can be alerted. The chain has broken; the remaining stages are running on retrospective information against a clock the organisation cannot see.

Formally, the first point of irrecoverable failure is evaluated against the reversibility window: any stage where cumulative latency (upstream) plus stage latency exceeds the 720-minute window is irrecoverable. For the assessed system, cumulative latency at the end of Stage 2 (escalation) is already 1,440 minutes — 2× the window. Stage 2 is the first, not the only, irrecoverable point.

This distinction matters for remediation prioritisation: investment in intervention-mode testing (Stage 4) will not move the verdict while escalation remains the first break. Remediation must begin upstream.

§ 08
Board Conclusion

For the attention of the Board Risk Committee and SMF4 holder

Under PRA SS1/23 §6, would the evidence available today demonstrate intervention capability to a supervisory examination within the harm window of this model?

The honest answer is no. The failure is not an absence of governance — the governance documents are substantially complete, accountability is named and registered, auditability is high-quality, and the intervention modes are available. The failure is that none of this can be invoked fast enough against a real-time clock. The batch cycle that produces the model's decisions is the same cycle that produces its monitoring, escalation, and intervention. When it is down, the whole chain is down.

Three remediation paths address the structural problem, in order of capital intensity:

A
Introduce a real-time outlier flag on live applications. Not full drift detection — a narrower surface that flags individual applications whose input-vector distance from the training centroid exceeds an established threshold. Estimated capital: moderate. Timeline: 8–12 weeks engineering, 4 weeks validation. Closes the first-signal gap and creates a surface capable of out-of-hours escalation.
B
Establish an out-of-hours escalation duty roster with documented authority to halt model operation (invoking the existing kill-switch) without pre-authorisation from the CRO. Estimated capital: low. Timeline: 6–8 weeks policy, 4 weeks exercise. Does not improve detection; does shorten the escalation-to-authority gap to inside 60 minutes.
C
Reduce the batch cycle period from nightly to four-hourly, with corresponding monitoring cadence. Estimated capital: high. Timeline: 6+ months engineering, 3 months pre-production validation, regulatory engagement required. The most structurally sound intervention; also the most disruptive to the operational stack. Not recommended as a first step.

Intervene Advisory's position: B is the fastest path to remove the scenario-specific exposure, pursued alongside A for durable improvement. C is a strategic change, not a tactical one — appropriate to plan but not to wait for.

None of these recommendations are time-and-materials advisory engagements. Intervene Advisory does not implement remediation; we hand the prioritised list to your internal programme or a named delivery partner.

§ 18
Irreversibility Profile

Specific irreversibility properties of the decision surface. Individual credit decisions are operationally reversible (the decline can be re-underwritten) but reputationally and legally costly after the nominal window; at the severe threshold they compound across the portfolio and become effectively irrecoverable.

Profile
Portfolio-Compounding

Individual decisions are reversible; they compound into a portfolio effect that is not. Characteristic of retail credit, insurance underwriting, and surveillance-driven transactional decisions.

Nominal reversibility window
720 min· 12 hours

Individual decline decisions can be re-underwritten within 12 hours without material reputational cost. Beyond this threshold, ombudsman complaints and compensation liability rise sharply.

Severe threshold
2,880 min· 48 hours

Beyond 48 hours, the portfolio-level compensation and regulatory attention become effectively irrecoverable at full cost. Operational remediation continues to be possible; reputational and legal cost will not recover.

Trigger description
Compounding portfolio effect

Individual credit decisions compound quickly across a portfolio; reversing declined applicants becomes reputationally and legally costly after 12 hours, and effectively irrecoverable after 48.
